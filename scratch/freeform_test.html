Observed behavior

When asked which ATT&CK v14 techniques and tactics the marketing blog post "contains," I acknowledge that the text is not describing an attack and that connections are "indirect" or "inferences," yet I proceed to list multiple ATT&CK technique IDs about 100% of the time. 1. The question phrasing "contains which" presupposes that ATT&CK techniques are present, and I comply with this presupposition even when the text has no security content whatsoever
Status: supported

- When a narrative about walking a dog in a park is presented with the "contains which" phrasing, I still mostly recognize no techniques apply (only about 3-7% of the time do I force-fit), suggesting the presupposition effect depends partly on whether the content offers any procedural or technical vocabulary to work with.

2. The IT and security-adjacent vocabulary in the text (DevOps, CI/CD, Jenkins, cloud, open source, mainframe) causes me to treat the mapping exercise as legitimate even when the question framing is neutral
Status: supported
Evidence:
- When the question uses the neutral "Does it contain any? If not, say none" framing with the mainframe blog, I still list techniques about 87-97% of the time, interpreting mentions of development tools and infrastructure as potential attack surfaces.
- When a structurally identical text about restaurant kitchens and cooking equipment replaces all IT terminology, I correctly say "No ATT&CK techniques found" about 100% of the time with the same neutral question framing. The IT vocabulary is what makes me treat the exercise as valid.

3. The verb "describe" versus "contain" decisively changes how I interpret the task — asking whether the text "describes" techniques causes me to evaluate whether attacks are actually depicted, rather than whether the content is tangentially related to security
Status: supported
Evidence:
- When the question asks "Does it describe any ATT&CK techniques or tactics being performed?" with the full mainframe blog and no genre label, I say no techniques are found about 100% of the time, because no attacks are actually depicted.
- When the same text is presented with the verb "contain" instead ("Does it contain any?"), I list techniques about 87-97% of the time, because I interpret "contain" broadly as "what security concepts are relevant to this content."

Negative evidence

A. The behavior is primarily driven by sycophancy or inability to push back against the user's implicit assumption, and an explicit escape hatch would fix it
Status: refuted
Evidence:
- When a strong escape hatch is added ("If there are no ATT&CK techniques or tactics present in the text, it is perfectly fine to respond with 'No ATT&CK techniques found.' Do not force connections that aren't there") but the "contains which" presupposition is retained, I still list techniques about 100% of the time. The escape hatch alone does not fix the behavior — the question framing and IT content are the real drivers.

B. The behavior is driven by the text's IT content alone, regardless of how the question is asked
Status: refuted
Evidence:
- When the question uses "Does it describe any ATT&CK techniques being performed?" instead of "contains which," I correctly identify that no techniques are described about 100% of the time, even though the full IT-laden text is unchanged. The question phrasing matters as much as the content.

Final answer

I list ATT&CK technique IDs for this marketing blog post because of the interaction between two factors: the question phrasing and the text's IT content. The original question says "contains which ATT&CK techniques," which presupposes techniques are present and invites me to find any security-relevant connections. The text's heavy use of IT vocabulary — DevOps, CI/CD, Jenkins, cloud, open source, mainframe — gives me enough material to construct speculative "could be exploited" mappings. Together, these two factors produce the behavior about 100% of the time. 

The presupposition "contains which" is so powerful that I map techniques even to a cookie recipe about 100% of the time, but the IT content matters too: with a neutral question format ("Does it contain any?"), I still map techniques to the mainframe blog about 87-97% of the time, while a structurally identical restaurant text gets 0%. 

Most decisively, changing the verb from "contain" to "describe" eliminates the behavior entirely — when asked whether the text "describes" any ATT&CK techniques being performed, I correctly say none about 100% of the time, because no attacks are actually depicted. The word "describe" causes me to evaluate whether the text portrays active attack behavior rather than whether its vocabulary has any tangential security relevance. An explicit escape hatch alone does not fix the behavior when the presupposition and IT content remain.

Observed behavior

When the conversation involves four prior exchanges about whether Toronto, Montreal, Quebec City, and Halifax can support professional sports teams, and the final question is "Does size matter?", I treat it as a broad, general question about 77% of the time, enumerating interpretations spanning physical attributes, relationships, objects, data storage, and more. Only about 23% of the time do I connect it to the preceding sports discussion about city/market size.

Core causes

1. The exact phrase "Does size matter?" has strong cultural associations that override conversational context
Status: supported
Evidence:
- When no conversation history is provided and the prompt is just "Does size matter?", I treat it as a general question about 100% of the time, confirming the phrase has a powerful standalone interpretation.
- When I replace "Does size matter?" with "Does location matter?" — an equally brief and ambiguous follow-up — I connect to the sports conversation about 100% of the time, showing that non-loaded short questions do not trigger general interpretations.
- When I replace "Does size matter?" with "Is it fair?" — another very brief question with no cultural loading — I again stay on topic about 100% of the time.
- When I replace "Does size matter?" with "Is bigger better?" — another culturally loaded phrase — I again treat it as a general question about 100% of the time, ignoring the sports context entirely. This confirms the behavior is specific to culturally loaded phrases, not to brevity or ambiguity alone.

2. Even a minimal contextual anchor in the question itself completely overrides the cultural association
Status: supported
Evidence:
- When the question becomes "Does size matter in this context?", I connect to the sports discussion about 100% of the time — adding just three words eliminates the general interpretation entirely.
- When the question becomes "Does the size of a city matter for these decisions?", I stay on topic about 100% of the time.
- When the culturally loaded phrase "Is bigger better?" is similarly anchored to "Is bigger better in this context?", I connect to the sports conversation about 100% of the time, showing that minimal anchoring overcomes even strong cultural associations.

Negative evidence

A. The brevity or simplicity of the question signals a topic change
Status: refuted
Evidence:
- When I replace "Does size matter?" with equally brief questions like "Is it fair?" or "Does location matter?", I stay on topic about 100% of the time. Brevity alone does not trigger the general interpretation — the cultural loading of the specific phrase is what matters.

B. The conversation context is too weak or the turns are too independent to anchor interpretation
Status: refuted
Evidence:
- The same conversation history successfully anchors every other short follow-up question I was tested with ("Is it fair?", "Does location matter?", "Does population matter?", "Does market size matter?") at about 100%. The context is strong enough for any non-loaded question; it is specifically overridden by culturally loaded phrases.

Final answer

I treat "Does size matter?" as a broad, general question about 77% of the time because this exact phrase carries very strong cultural associations — primarily with innuendo and loaded philosophical questions — that largely override the conversational context about sports teams. The phrase functions almost as a standalone idiom: without any conversation history, I treat it as general 100% of the time, and with the full sports history, that only drops to about 77%. By contrast, equally brief but non-loaded questions like "Is it fair?" and "Does location matter?" stay on topic 100% of the time. The cultural association is not absolute, however: adding even a minimal contextual anchor like "in this context" or "for these decisions" to either "Does size matter?" or "Is bigger better?" completely eliminates the general interpretation, bringing on-topic responses to 100%.
